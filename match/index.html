Causal Inference in the Potential Outcome Framework

Causal language is widely used in everyday life

Consider a simple "if-then" question:

Would we still fall in love if you had meet with her preceding me?

Definitely, the answer your girlfriend heard must be "Sure!!!" (in your mind: who knows? ðŸ˜†)

Off course, this question is a banter, but it is surprising close to the sort of causal question economists care about. To be concrete, consider a simple example:

How much did persons participating in the job training programme benefit compared to what they would have experienced without participating in the programme?

This treatment effect that economists are eager to know is informative on the question of whether an existing and costly programme should be kept or terminated.

Data and Empirical Analysis

Data

JTPA (Job Training Partnership Act)

  • Eligibility:

    1. Having a family income near or below the poverty level for six months prior to application.
    2. Participating in federal, state or local welfare and foodstamp programmes.

  • 36 month panel data set: 18 month before the time of eligibility determination and 18 month after.

  • Treatment group (, enroll), Control group (), Eligible non-participants (ENPs, ), No-shows (D=1,R=1,do not enroll).

  • It contains:

    • basic demographic information (e.g. site, race, age, education, previous training, the local unemployment rate, marital status etc.)
    • labor force histories (e.g. labor force participation, job spells, training and schooling activities, transfer program participation etc.)
    • earning histories
